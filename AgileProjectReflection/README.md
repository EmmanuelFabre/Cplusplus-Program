I interpret user needs by breaking them down into user stories with clear acceptance criteria, so vague requests become actionable work. In the SNHU Travel project, when the stakeholder pivoted to wellness travel, the Product Owner and I refined the story through backlog refinement and direct communication until we had a concrete definition of what "wellness destination" meant, and what specifically needed to change. Creating user stories helped because they forced us to separate what the user actually needed (updated content) from what we assumed they needed (a complete rebuild). Without that story structure, the developer would have scrapped working code unnecessarily. User stories turn ambiguous business goals into scoped, testable deliverables that the whole team can align on.

I approach development iteratively, building in short sprints, getting feedback, and adjusting iteratively. As for being a good team member, it comes down to accountability and communication. Every role on our team succeeded because people owned their responsibilities and spoke up when something was unclear. My follow-up email to Christy and Brian is an example. I didn't wait for answers to come to me, I asked targeted questions, directed them to the right people, and committed to a timeline. A good team member also creates psychological safety so others feel comfortable doing the same, because when people stay quiet out of fear, the whole team slows down.
